01

Executive summary

What a partner or investor should understand quickly.

`hejkohi` is a pre-launch coffee house concept designed to sit between chain convenience and narrow specialty coffee culture. The idea is to build a room people actively choose when they want to slow down, stay longer, and return often, rather than simply buy caffeine and leave.

Business overview

The first `hejkohi` location should operate as a flagship neighborhood coffee house with a compact, high-quality menu, strong atmosphere, and a guest experience built around repeat visits. The concept is not positioned as a novelty venue or a trend-driven design object. It is meant to become part of a customer’s weekly routine while still feeling distinct and premium.

Why this concept matters

The market is crowded with coffee businesses, but much of it splits between speed-led operators and craft-led specialty bars. `hejkohi` is intended to serve the space in between: design-aware, emotionally memorable, operationally disciplined, and broad enough to attract everyday customers rather than only enthusiasts.

What the first site must prove

  • That the concept can build a base of repeat local customers rather than one-time curiosity.
  • That premium everyday pricing feels justified by atmosphere, product quality, and service.
  • That a focused menu and lean team can produce healthy unit economics without harming the guest experience.
  • That the brand has enough emotional clarity to support word of mouth and future expansion.

Capital objective

The business plan is aimed at finding either a founding partner or an early investor who can help finance the first flagship location and contribute useful commercial or hospitality experience. The immediate objective is not aggressive scaling. It is to open one strong first site and validate a repeatable model.

02

Company description

What the business is, what it sells, and what stage it is in.

`hejkohi` is currently a concept-stage hospitality business with no operating history. The company is being developed around the launch of one flagship coffee house, with future multi-site potential only after the first location is proven.

Business model

Revenue is expected to come primarily from dine-in and takeaway coffee and tea, supported by bakery, limited savory items, and a smaller layer of retail such as beans, tea, or simple branded objects. The model should be deliberately restrained: enough offer to support average ticket growth, but not so much complexity that the room becomes hard to run.

Customer offer

The business is not selling coffee alone. It is selling a reliable daily ritual: high product standards, comfortable stay quality, and a room that feels calm, tactile, and considered. That combination is the core of the proposition.

Current stage

Today the company should be described as pre-launch. The brand thesis exists, the hospitality concept is defined at a working level, and the next steps are site selection, founder-story refinement, detailed market research, and financial modeling grounded in a real city and shell.

Legal structure

Assumption

If the first site launches in Germany, a founder-led `GmbH` or similar limited liability structure is likely the cleanest vehicle for investment, lease signing, and operating risk management. Final legal form should be decided with local tax and legal advice once the first city and investor path are clear.

04

Market opportunity

Why the gap is believable and where the first site should compete.

The opportunity is not “coffee is a big market.” The opportunity is that many coffee businesses are optimized for speed, craft signaling, or visual novelty, while fewer are built around emotionally durable repeat behavior.

Problem / market gap

Chains win on convenience and predictability. Specialty operators win on coffee credibility. Design-led cafes often win on image. `hejkohi` is meant to win on premium everyday habit: a place people choose because it feels good to be there and easy to come back to.

Target customer

The primary customers are local professionals, creative workers, and neighborhood residents who want a dependable morning or midday ritual. Secondary customers are slower social visitors who meet, stay longer, and attach food or a second drink. The strongest guest is not the trend-seeker; it is the repeat customer who decides this is the default place to go.

Location strategy

Assumption

The best first site is likely in a walkable, mixed residential and destination neighborhood with visible frontage, design-aware foot traffic, and enough daily density to support weekday routine. Hamburg appears to be a strong candidate because it fits the founder context and the concept’s pace, but that remains a working assumption until rent, footfall, and competitor research confirm it.

05

Product, experience & marketing/sales

What customers buy, how the room feels, and how demand is built.

`hejkohi` should make money because customers want to return, not because the menu is oversized. The product, room, and marketing strategy must support the same behavior: discovery, first affection, and then routine.

Product / menu

The opening menu should center on espresso drinks, milk drinks, one slower brew format, matcha or tea, bakery, and a limited set of savory items. Retail should remain selective: enough to deepen the brand and extend average ticket, but not enough to distract from the core hospitality offer.

Guest experience

The room should be warm, tactile, and calm. Seating needs to support both short and longer stays. Service should feel attentive without performance. Sound level, lighting, materials, ceramics, bathroom quality, and table reset all matter because they make the concept tangible.

Marketing and sales plan

The first marketing engine should be local and physical: storefront visibility, neighborhood curiosity, local introductions, and the room’s ability to convert first-time visitors into repeat customers. Digital channels should amplify what is already true in the room rather than manufacture a false idea of demand.

  • Launch with local-first storytelling and soft-opening invitations.
  • Use photography and video that show the space honestly rather than over-styled campaign material.
  • Build presence through Google Maps, Instagram, community word of mouth, and small collaborations.
  • Keep sales channels simple at launch: dine-in, takeaway, and later limited preorder if operations justify it.

Community strategy

The brand should become part of the neighborhood by collaborating with adjacent local culture: ceramics, books, print, baking, or small events that fit the room’s pace. The goal is not event programming for its own sake. The goal is to make `hejkohi` feel embedded, useful, and worth returning to.

06

Operations

How the concept stays calm in front and disciplined behind the scenes.

Good hospitality depends on operational clarity. `hejkohi` should feel soft to the guest but be run with strong standards, simple flows, and a menu that matches the team’s real capacity.

Operating model

The operating model should favor clarity over range. That means compact prep, strong bar flow, manageable opening hours, and an intentional balance between takeaway efficiency and a room that encourages people to stay.

Staffing

Assumption

The first site should launch with a small, standards-driven team: a founder or working manager, core baristas, and flexible support around peak periods. The team should be large enough to protect guest experience and small enough to stay financially disciplined.

Site, equipment, and sourcing

The ideal shell supports 30 to 40 seats, strong frontage, straightforward bar service, and limited kitchen dependency. Suppliers should be chosen for consistency, not just story value. Coffee, tea, bakery, and selected food items must all be repeatable within the staffing model.

Quality control

Product standards, cleaning routines, training checklists, opening and closing procedures, and service language should all be documented. If the room is part of the product, then operational sloppiness is not a side issue. It is a direct brand failure.

07

Launch plan

How the flagship should move from concept to stable operation.

The launch should be staged carefully. A strong opening is not defined by noise. It is defined by how quickly the business reaches a repeatable, calm operating rhythm.

Phase 1: site, legal, and build preparation

Secure the site, finalize legal structure, complete financial model, align on design intent, gather contractor quotes, and confirm equipment and supplier needs. The output of this phase is a fully budgeted launch plan tied to a real shell.

Phase 2: build-out, hiring, and training

Fit-out, equipment installation, recipe testing, hiring, and staff training happen here. By the end of this phase, the room should be functional, the menu should be narrowed to launch-ready items, and service routines should already feel practiced.

Phase 3: soft launch and public opening

A soft launch should invite neighbors, friends of the brand, and local community contacts before a bigger public push. The purpose is to observe queue flow, dwell behavior, ticket mix, and where the room or menu still creates friction.

Milestones

  • First 30 days: service consistency, staff rhythm, and early feedback loops.
  • First 90 days: better visibility into daypart demand, attachment, and labor planning.
  • 6 months: a clearer view of repeat-customer strength and true neighborhood fit.
  • 12 months: enough evidence to judge whether the flagship is turning into a repeatable business model.

Financial plan

How the business makes money and what still needs numeric proof.

The financial plan should be rigorous but honest. At this stage, the structure of the model is clear, but the final numbers should only be locked once the target city, site size, and rent realities are known.

Revenue model

The core revenue engine is beverage-led, with bakery and light food increasing average ticket and dwell value. A smaller retail layer may support margin and brand extension, but the business should not depend on retail to make the model work.

Cost structure

The major cost buckets are fit-out and equipment, rent and deposit, labor, ingredients, utilities, and working capital. The concept only remains attractive if atmosphere is achieved with financial discipline rather than expensive perfectionism.

Current financial analysis

`hejkohi` is a startup concept and currently has no operating revenue, no historical cost base, and no proven asset performance. The present value of the project lies in the concept, the founder’s vision, the brand positioning, and the potential to build a flagship with durable local demand.

09

Investment opportunity

What capital is needed and what kind of investor makes sense.

The investment case for `hejkohi` is not hypergrowth. It is the chance to back a thoughtful, premium everyday concept at the flagship stage and help shape a business that may become repeatable once the first site is proven.

Use of funds

Capital should be used for lease deposit, fit-out, espresso and bar equipment, furniture and lighting, initial inventory, hiring and training, pre-opening costs, and a working-capital buffer strong enough to let the team learn without panic.

Raise structure

Assumption

The cleanest early structure is likely a minority equity investment or convertible instrument tied to the first flagship. A revenue-share structure could be considered, but only if it does not create pressure for short-term extraction at the expense of brand and operational discipline.

Ideal investor profile

The ideal investor is not only writing a check. They bring patience, practical hospitality or consumer-business judgment, and comfort with a first-location proof model. Value beyond capital could include real-estate access, hospitality mentorship, strategic operations advice, or disciplined support through site selection and launch.

Value creation logic

Investor value will come from building a flagship with strong repeat behavior, stable unit economics, and a credible case for measured expansion. The first milestone is not “open a second store fast.” It is “make the first store undeniably real.”

10

Risks, open research & appendix

What still needs proof and how the plan should stay intellectually honest.

A credible business plan names the risks plainly. `hejkohi` is still early, so clarity around open questions is an asset, not a weakness.

Main risks

  • Site risk: the room could be right but the location commercially weak.
  • Demand risk: the brand may attract attention without building repeat behavior.
  • Build-cost risk: the desire for quality could push capex too high.
  • Operational drift: a beautiful concept could become inconsistent in execution.
  • Staffing risk: small hospitality teams are vulnerable if hiring or training is weak.

Mitigation approach

These risks are best managed by narrowing the menu, being ruthless about site criteria, building a realistic budget with contingency, documenting service standards early, and not scaling until the first site genuinely performs.
